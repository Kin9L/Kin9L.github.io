Reinforce Algorithm

Posted on 2022-03-26 Edited on 2022-03-31

Introduction

The Reinforce is a kind of policy-based reinforcement learning algorithm. The policy can be regarded as a function, which can output the action‘s probabilistic with the state as its input. In Reinforce, we can use a neural network to formulate the policy, termed as $a\sim\pi_\theta(s)$. With the help of policy gradient, parameters of the policy can be optimized to make it perform better.

Policy Gradient in Reinforce

The goal of the Reinforce is to find a parameterized policy $\pi_{\theta}$ to make the cumulative reward maximum, as shown in Eq. (1). The cumulative reward $R(\tau)$ is shown in Eq. (2). Generally, the gradient ascent method can be used to optimize the $\theta$, as shown in Eq. (3). However, the function $R(\tau)$ is nondifferential related to $\theta$ because it is directly obtained from the environment. How to solve this problem?

Assume that there is a function $f(x)$, and $x$ is sampled from a parameterized probabilistic distribution $p(x|\theta)$. Use $x$ to substitute $\tau$ in Eq. (1), and use $f(x)$ to substitute $R(\tau)$, as shown in Eq. (4). Take the derivative of $\theta$ on both sides of Eq. (4) to get Eq. (4). According to the definition of the expectation and the Leibniz formula, Eq. (6) can be obtained from Eq. (5). Multiply a item $\frac{p(x|\theta)}{p(x|\theta)}$ inside the Eq. (6), so that the logarithmic differentiation can be applied. According to the definition of the expectation, Eq. (7) is obtained.

Use $\tau$ and $R(\tau)$ to substitute $x$ and $f(x)$ respectively in the Eq. (7) to get the Eq. (8). The item $p(\tau|\theta)$ can be associated with the policy $\pi$, as shown in Eq. (9). Take the logarithm on both sides of Eq. (9) to obtain the Eq. (10). Take the derivative of $\theta$ on both sides of Eq. (10) to obtain the Eq. (11). Then substitute Eq. (11) into Eq. (8) to obtain Eq. (12). Eq. (12) solves the problem that we cannot calculate the derivative of $\theta$ in Eq. (1). Finally, the derivation of $\theta$ on $J(\theta)$ can be obtained from Eq. (13). With help of Monte Carlo Sampling, the expectation in Eq. (13) can be calculated as the average of numbers of trajectories. In Reinforce, only one trajectory is used to calculate the expectation, as shown in Eq. (14). This procedure can make the calculation easier. However, it also weakens the stability of the Reinforce. The policy collapse might occur in Reinforce which makes its performance worse.
